From Malaria Drug to Anticancer Agent: A
Five-Year Review of Artemisinin's
Evolving Role in Oncology (2021-2026
Molecular Mechanisms of Action: The Dominance of
Ferroptosis and Beyond
The investigation into artemisinin and its derivatives as anticancer agents has matured
significantly over the past five years, shifting from broad observations of cytotoxicity to a
deeply nuanced understanding of their specific molecular mechanisms. While the
foundational principle of iron-dependent activation remains central, recent research has
firmly established iron-dependent programmed cell death, or ferroptosis, as the dominant
pathway through which these compounds exert their selective toxicity against malignant
cells 2 3 . This mechanistic clarity provides a robust framework for rationalizing
therapeutic applications and designing novel strategies to enhance their efficacy. The
sesquiterpene lactone core of artemisinin, particularly its essential 1,2,4-trioxane
pharmacophore containing an endoperoxide bridge, is the chemical basis for all its
biological activities 2 6 . This peroxide bridge is activated upon interaction with
intracellular iron species, primarily heme or free ferrous iron (Fe²⁺), leading to its
cleavage and the generation of cytotoxic carbon-centered free radicals and a subsequent
surge of reactive oxygen species (ROS) 10 14 21 . This oxidative burst is the initial trigger
for a cascade of downstream cellular damage that culminates in multiple forms of
regulated cell death.
The induction of ferroptosis represents a paradigm shift in understanding artemisinin's
action. Unlike classical apoptosis, ferroptosis is characterized by the iron-dependent
accumulation of lethal levels of lipid peroxides on cellular membranes, leading to
membrane rupture and cell death 2 3 . Artemisinins are potent inducers of this
pathway, acting at several critical junctures. A primary mechanism involves the
disruption of intracellular iron homeostasis. Studies have shown that artemisinins
promote the lysosomal degradation of ferritin, the principal protein responsible for
storing intracellular iron 2

3 . This process, termed ferritinophagy, results in the release

of a large pool of free iron into the cytosol 2

6 . The sudden increase in labile iron fuels

the Fenton reaction, a chemical process where Fe²⁺ catalyzes the conversion of less

reactive hydrogen peroxide into highly destructive hydroxyl radicals 2 . These radicals
initiate and propagate the peroxidation of polyunsaturated fatty acids within cellular
membranes, causing catastrophic oxidative damage 2

3 .

Crucially, artemisinins simultaneously disarm the cell's primary defense system against
lipid peroxidation. The glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4) enzyme, often referred to as the
guardian of ferroptosis, relies on glutathione (GSH) to neutralize lipid hydroperoxides
and repair membrane damage 2 . Artemisinins can downregulate the system Xctransporter, which imports cystine for GSH synthesis, and directly inhibit GPX4 activity
2

4 . By crippling this antioxidant axis, artemisinins push the cell beyond its capacity to

manage oxidative stress, sealing its fate through ferroptosis 2 7 . This dual action—
flooding the cell with pro-oxidant iron while disabling its anti-oxidant machinery—is a
hallmark of artemisinin's ferroptotic mechanism. The sensitivity of cancer cells to this
process is often heightened due to their "iron addiction," a state of elevated intracellular
iron required for rapid proliferation, making them more vulnerable than normal cells 9
14 .

While ferroptosis is the principal mode of action, artemisinins engage a complex network
of ancillary mechanisms that collectively contribute to their anti-tumor efficacy.
Apoptosis, a form of caspase-dependent programmed cell death, is frequently induced as
a secondary effect 1 3 . The initial ROS burst and DNA damage caused by artemisininderived radicals can activate intrinsic apoptotic pathways, leading to mitochondrial outer
membrane permeabilization, cytochrome c release, and the activation of executioner
caspases like caspase-3 3 14 . For instance, exposure of HepG2 liver cancer cells to
artemisinins resulted in a dose-dependent increase in caspase-3 cleavage, confirming the
involvement of apoptosis 3 . Both ROS-dependent and ROS-independent apoptotic
pathways have been documented, indicating the versatility of this mechanism 3 .
Autophagy, a process of cellular self-degradation, is another pathway modulated by
artemisinins, though its role is context-dependent and described as "double-edged" 21 . In
some scenarios, autophagy serves as a pro-survival mechanism, allowing cancer cells to
recycle damaged components and resist cell death. However, in other contexts, it can
proceed to a point where it becomes a form of programmed cell death itself 3 .
Dihydroartemisinin (DHA) has been shown to induce autophagy in leukemia K562 cells
and breast cancer cells, evidenced by increased LC3-II protein expression and the
degradation of p62/SQSTM1 3 . In breast cancer cells, this autophagic response was
dependent on ROS generation and could even sensitize the cells to chemotherapeutic

agents like epirubicin 3 . Conversely, in glioma cells, DHA induces autophagy, but
whether this promotes survival or death requires further clarification 14 .
Cell cycle arrest is a consistent and well-documented effect of artemisinin treatment
across a wide array of cancer cell lines 1 3 . Artemisinins can halt the proliferation of
cancer cells by inducing arrest at specific checkpoints. A predominant finding is the
induction of G1-phase cell cycle arrest, observed in gallbladder, prostate, lung,
pancreatic, hepatoma, ovarian, and endometrial cancer cell lines 3 . This arrest is
typically mediated by the downregulation of key regulatory proteins, including cyclindependent kinase 4 (CDK4) and cyclin D1, which are essential for the transition from the
G1 phase to the S phase of the cell cycle 3 . In contrast, G2/M-phase arrest has been
reported in osteosarcoma, breast cancer, renal carcinoma, and neuroblastoma cells 3 .
For example, artemisinin was found to cause G2/M-phase arrest in breast cancer cells, an
effect that was linked to its regulation of autophagy 3 . This ability to halt cell division
provides a crucial anti-proliferative effect alongside direct cytotoxic actions.
Furthermore, artemisinins exhibit potent anti-angiogenic activity, which is critical for
inhibiting tumor growth and metastasis 12 . Tumors require the formation of new blood
vessels (angiogenesis) to supply nutrients and oxygen for expansion beyond a minimal
size. Artemisinins disrupt this process by modulating key signaling pathways and factors
involved in angiogenesis 12 . One major mechanism involves the inhibition of the NF-κB
transcription factor, a master regulator of inflammation and pro-survival genes 3 . By
suppressing NF-κB, artemisinins downregulate the expression of a suite of downstream
pro-angiogenic factors, including vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF),
interleukin-8 (IL-8), cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), and matrix metalloproteinase-9 (MMP-9)
3 . Dihydroartemisinin (DHA) has demonstrated significant anti-angiogenic effects in

both in vitro models and in vivo pancreatic cancer-derived tumors 3 . Similarly,
artesunate (ART) has been shown to inhibit VEGF expression, highlighting its potential
utility in treating hyper-vascularized cancers such as Kaposi’s sarcoma 3 . By starving the
tumor of its blood supply, artemisinins can exert a powerful indirect anti-tumor effect.
The complexity of these mechanisms is underscored by their modulation through various
signaling pathways. For instance, artemisinins can regulate the PI3K/AKT/mTOR
pathway, which is a central hub for cell growth and survival, and inhibit STAT3 signaling,
a transcription factor often constitutively active in cancers 5 8 . They also activate
AMPK signaling, which acts as an energy sensor and can suppress anabolic processes like
cell proliferation 8 . In lung cancer, DHA has been shown to inhibit STAT3 signaling 5 ,
while in epithelial ovarian cancer, it inhibits the hedgehog signaling pathway 1 . In

prostate cancer cells, artesunate suppresses viability and mobility through the UCA1/
miR-184 axis, where a long non-coding RNA (UCA1) acts as a sponge for a microRNA
(miR-184) 1 . This intricate web of interactions demonstrates that artemisinins do not
act through a single monolithic pathway but rather perturb multiple interconnected
nodes within the cancer cell's signaling network, leading to a multi-pronged attack on
malignancy. This promiscuous targeting of over 300 specific proteins in colorectal cancer
cells further supports the idea that their efficacy stems from overwhelming the cell's
adaptive capacity 9 .

This comprehensive mechanistic profile reveals why artemisinin is considered a versatile
tool in oncology. Its ability to trigger multiple, convergent death pathways makes it
difficult for cancer cells to develop resistance through a single mutation. The dominance
of ferroptosis, however, offers a clear and actionable target. Future therapeutic strategies
will likely focus on identifying biomarkers that predict a tumor's susceptibility to this
form of cell death, such as high baseline iron levels, low GPX4 expression, or mutations
in key regulators like p53, which can sensitize cells to ferroptosis 2 . Understanding
these context-dependent nuances is paramount for moving artemisinin therapy from a
generalized cytotoxic approach to a more precise and effective anticancer strategy.

Clinical Translation and Efficacy Outcomes in Human
Trials
The translation of artemisinin's promising preclinical activity into tangible clinical
benefits has been a methodical and cautious process, with research from 2021 to 2026
primarily focused on establishing safety profiles and exploring niche applications in
advanced or pre-malignant conditions. While large-scale efficacy trials demonstrating
survival benefits are still awaited, a growing body of evidence from early-phase human
studies provides valuable insights into the potential of these compounds. The clinical
journey has largely followed two paths: investigating intravenous (IV) administration for
refractory solid tumors and evaluating oral formulations as add-on therapy for specific
cancers or as chemopreventive agents. The collective data indicate that artemisinins are
generally well-tolerated, but their efficacy as monotherapy appears modest, suggesting
their greatest potential lies in combination regimens or specific patient populations.
Phase I clinical trials have been instrumental in defining the safety, tolerability, and
pharmacokinetics of both IV and oral artemisinin formulations. For intravenous
artesunate (IV AS), a pivotal Phase I accelerated titration study enrolled 19 patients with
advanced solid malignancies to determine the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) 14 15 .
Patients received escalating doses of artesunate (from 8 mg/kg up to 45 mg/kg) on days
1 and 8 of a 21-day cycle 15 . Dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs) were observed at doses of 12
mg/kg, 18 mg/kg, and 25 mg/kg, including neutropenic fever, hypersensitivity reactions,
liver function test abnormalities, and severe nausea/vomiting 15 21 . Based on these
findings, the MTD was established at 18 mg/kg 3 14 15 . At this dose, the maximum
plasma concentration (Cmax) was 415 ng/ml, and the treatment was deemed generally
well-tolerated 3 . Notably, auditory and vestibular system adverse events were noted but
were reversible upon discontinuation of the drug 21 . Another trial confirmed the MTD at
18 mg/kg, reinforcing this as a safe dose level for further investigation 21 .
In parallel, studies on oral artemisinin and its derivatives have established different safety
benchmarks. A prospective open-label Phase I study evaluated escalating daily oral doses
of artemisinin (up to 200 mg/d, equivalent to 2.2-3.9 mg/kg/d) in patients with
advanced non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) 16 . The study concluded that doses up to
200 mg/day were safe and well-tolerated, and this dose was recommended for
consideration in future Phase II/III trials 16 . Similarly, multiple Phase I studies
conducted in patients with metastatic breast cancer investigated oral artesunate at doses
of 100 mg/day, 150 mg/day, and 200 mg/day 14 . These studies consistently found the
treatment to be well-tolerated over periods ranging from four weeks to over three years

in compassionate use settings 14 18 21 . Any dose-limiting adverse events observed were
completely reversible upon cessation of the drug 14 . However, one compassionate use
study did report that the incidence of grade ≥2 adverse events increased with dosage,
rising from two events in the 100 mg group to six in the 150 mg group and seventeen in
the 200 mg group, although no event was considered definitively related to artesunate
21 . These findings suggest that while oral artemisinins have a favorable safety profile,

higher doses may be associated with an increased burden of side effects.
Despite the establishment of safe dosing levels, objective tumor responses have been rare
in these early-phase trials, pointing towards a more subtle role for artemisinins in cancer
management. In the Phase I trial of IV artesunate in refractory solid tumors, no complete
or partial remissions were observed 15 . However, four patients achieved stable disease,
resulting in a disease control rate of 27% and including three patients with prolonged
stable disease lasting 8, 10, and 11 cycles 15 . This suggests that artesunate can
temporarily halt tumor progression in a subset of patients. A similar pattern was seen in
NSCLC, where adding oral artesunate at 120 mg/day to standard vinorelbine plus
cisplatin chemotherapy failed to significantly improve short-term or mean survival 3 .
Nevertheless, the artesunate group showed a significantly higher disease control rate and
a longer time to disease progression, hinting at a disease-stabilizing effect 3 .
In metastatic breast cancer, a Phase I study of oral artesunate found that after four weeks
of treatment, 10 out of 23 patients had stable disease, but again, no complete or partial
remissions were recorded 3 . This aligns with the concept of artemisinins as agents that
control rather than eradicate disease in heavily pre-treated populations. A particularly
interesting finding came from a Phase I dose-finding study in women with cervical
intraepithelial neoplasia grade 2/3 (CIN2/3), a pre-malignant condition 3 . After
treatment with intravaginal artemisinin, histologic regression to CIN1 or less was
observed in 68% of subjects, with no intolerable side effects reported 3 . This highlights
a potentially significant role for artemisinins in chemoprevention or the treatment of
early-stage lesions.
Perhaps the most compelling signal of efficacy comes from a randomized, double-blind,
placebo-controlled pilot study in colorectal cancer (CRC) patients 3 14 . In this study,
patients received oral artesunate at 200 mg/day for 14 days 5 . Bayesian analysis
revealed a high probability (97%) of a decrease in the tumor proliferation marker Ki-67
and a moderate probability (79%) of increased microvessel density (CD31 expression) 3
14 . More strikingly, during a follow-up period, the recurrence-free survival probability

was dramatically higher in the artesunate group compared to the placebo group at both 2

and 3 years (0.89 vs. 0.5) 3 14 . No deaths were reported in the artesunate group versus
three in the placebo group during the study period 14 . While preliminary, these results
strongly suggest that adjuvant artesunate therapy may reduce the risk of CRC recurrence
and warrant larger confirmatory trials.
Other clinical investigations have yielded mixed or negative results. A clinical controlled
trial (CCT) for prostate cancer assessed oral artemisinin (300–400 mg, t.i.d.) over three
years, with outcomes measured by PSA doubling time and signs of metastasis, but the
provided sources do not contain the final results of this trial 5 . A CCT for metastatic
breast cancer evaluated oral artesunate at 100, 150, and 200 mg/day for four weeks,
focusing on safety and tolerability, but again, the detailed efficacy outcomes are not
specified in the provided text 5 . A pilot study of Artenimol-R (oral artemisinin) in 28
patients with advanced cervical cancer reported clinical remission with a median
symptom disappearance time of 7 days, indicating good tolerability with no grade 3 or 4
adverse events 14 .

In summary, the clinical evidence accumulated between 2021 and 2026 positions
artemisinins as relatively safe agents whose primary demonstrated value is in disease
stabilization and prevention rather than tumor eradication. The lack of robust objective
responses in monotherapy settings for advanced cancers underscores the need for more

sophisticated application strategies. The most encouraging data come from niche
indications, such as the reduction of recurrence in colorectal cancer and the regression of
pre-malignant cervical lesions. These findings provide a strong rationale for advancing
artemisinins into larger, randomized Phase II and III trials, particularly as adjunctive
therapy in the adjuvant setting and in combination with conventional treatments where
they may act as sensitizing agents. The path forward requires a shift from proving basic
safety to validating these promising signals of efficacy in rigorously designed clinical
studies.

Synergistic Combination Therapies and Overcoming
Resistance
Recognizing the limitations of artemisinin monotherapy, a significant portion of recent
research has focused on leveraging its unique mechanisms of action within synergistic
combination regimens. The goal is to enhance efficacy, overcome intrinsic or acquired
resistance, and broaden the therapeutic window. Preclinical and early clinical data
overwhelmingly support the concept that artemisinins can potentiate the effects of a wide
range of anticancer agents, including conventional chemotherapeutics, targeted
therapies, and radiotherapy. This strategic integration positions artemisinins not as
standalone cures but as versatile partners in multimodal cancer treatment protocols.
A large body of evidence demonstrates synergistic interactions between artemisinins and
standard chemotherapeutic drugs. Synergy, defined as a Combination Index (CI) of less
than 1, has been observed in numerous in vitro and in vivo models 3 . For example,
dihydroartemisinin (DHA) combined with gemcitabine showed synergistic effects in both
ovarian and pancreatic cancer cells 3 . In pancreatic cancer, DHA was also found to work
synergistically with Apo2L/TRAIL, a death receptor ligand, by up-regulating death
receptor 5 in a ROS-dependent manner 1 3 . Perhaps one of the most compelling
examples is the combination of artesunate (ART) and sorafenib in hepatocellular
carcinoma (HCC) 3 . This pairing enhances the anticancer effect by inducing oxidative
stress and promoting lysosome-mediated ferritinophagy, leading to the release of lethal
iron stores that fuel ferroptosis 3 21 . In acute myeloid leukemia (AML), ART combined
with bortezomib exhibited synergistic anti-proliferative effects 3 . Furthermore, DHAcarboplatin conjugates have been developed to overcome platinum resistance in ovarian
cancer cells, representing a more advanced approach to combination therapy 21 .

Artemisinins also show great promise in overcoming chemoresistance, a major obstacle in
cancer treatment. In colorectal cancer, DHA was shown to restore sensitivity to 5fluorouracil (5-FU) in TP53-deficient cells, which are often notoriously resistant to
therapy 14 . A synergistic combination of ART and halofuginone (HF) promoted caspasedependent apoptosis in both parental and oxaliplatin-resistant CRC cells, suggesting its
potential to re-sensitize tumors to platinum-based chemotherapy 14 . In esophageal
cancer, DHA attenuated the sonic hedgehog (Shh) signaling pathway, which is often
activated by cisplatin and contributes to resistance. By inhibiting this pathway, DHA
effectively sensitized cisplatin-resistant ESCC cells to the chemotherapy 14 . This ability to
target and inhibit survival pathways that are upregulated in response to chemotherapy is
a key advantage of incorporating artemisinins into treatment schedules.
Another critical area of synergy is with targeted therapies. The combination of
osimertinib, a third-generation tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) for EGFR-mutant non-small
cell lung cancer (NSCLC), with dihydroartemisinin (DHA) has been proposed as a novel
therapeutic strategy 13 . This repurposing approach aims to exploit the complementary
mechanisms of these agents to achieve a more durable response. In liver cancer, the
synergy between ART and sorafenib, another multikinase inhibitor, has been highlighted,
where the combination induces ferroptosis 3 21 . In prostate cancer, DHA was found to
synergize with docetaxel, a standard chemotherapy, to enhance survival rates in
xenograft mouse models, offering a potential strategy to improve outcomes in metastatic
castration-resistant disease 14 .
The ability of artemisinins to act as potent radiosensitizers is another important avenue of
research. Radiation therapy kills cancer cells primarily by inducing DNA damage, and
artemisinins can amplify this effect. Artesunate has been shown to enhance the
radiosensitivity of esophageal cancer cells by inhibiting the repair of radiation-induced
DNA damage 5 . This suggests that artemisinins could be used concurrently with
radiotherapy to allow for lower radiation doses, thereby reducing normal tissue toxicity,
or to increase the efficacy of standard radiation protocols. This property is particularly
relevant given the central role of radiation in the treatment of many solid tumors.
Beyond direct synergistic killing, innovative strategies are being developed to enhance
the selectivity and potency of artemisinins themselves. A particularly clever approach
involves exploiting the metabolic differences between cancer and normal cells. In
colorectal cancer (CRC) cells, which have a high capacity for heme synthesis, artemisinin
activation is significantly greater than in normal colon epithelial cells 9 . Researchers
have leveraged this by co-administering aminolevulinic acid (ALA), a clinically used
precursor in the heme biosynthesis pathway. In HCT116 CRC cells, adding ALA to

artesunate treatment lowered the IC50 from approximately 2 μM to just 200 nM, a tenfold increase in potency 9 . This combination preferentially increased intracellular heme
levels and ART activation specifically within the cancer cells, leading to significant tumor
growth delay in a mouse xenograft model compared to either agent alone 9 . This
strategy effectively turns a cancer cell's own metabolic vulnerability against it,
dramatically improving the therapeutic index of artemisinin.

These findings collectively argue that the future of artemisinin-based oncology lies in
intelligent combination strategies. Rather than viewing artemisinins as a replacement for
existing therapies, they should be seen as powerful adjuvants that can make conventional
treatments more effective, help overcome resistance, and potentially reduce side effects.
The development of these combinations requires careful preclinical validation to identify
optimal dosing schedules and sequences. For instance, sequencing matters when
combining agents that induce DNA damage with those that affect DNA repair. The most
successful clinical applications will likely emerge from personalized combination
regimens tailored to the specific genetic and metabolic vulnerabilities of an individual's
tumor.

Novel Derivatives and Advanced Delivery Systems
A major thrust of contemporary artemisinin research is dedicated to overcoming the
inherent pharmacological limitations of the natural compounds, namely their poor
aqueous solubility, short plasma half-life, and limited tissue distribution 3 . To address
these challenges and unlock the full therapeutic potential of the artemisinin scaffold,
scientists are pursuing two parallel and highly innovative strategies: the rational design
of novel synthetic derivatives and hybrids, and the development of advanced
nanoformulations for targeted drug delivery. These efforts aim to create next-generation
artemisinin-based therapeutics with improved potency, selectivity, and pharmacokinetic
profiles.
The creation of novel chemical entities derived from the artemisinin structure is a rapidly
expanding field. Researchers are employing medicinal chemistry principles to modify the
parent molecule to enhance its anticancer properties. One prominent strategy is the
synthesis of artemisinin dimers, which have been shown to be significantly more potent
than their monomeric counterparts, sometimes exhibiting activity several orders of
magnitude higher 10 . A prime example is ZQJ29, a novel cyano-substituted artemisinin
dimer designed for pancreatic cancer 7 . In vitro studies revealed that ZQJ29 possessed
extraordinary cytotoxicity against PANC-1 and KP4 pancreatic cancer cell lines, with an
IC50 of 0.120 µM, representing approximately 100-fold greater potency than the
standard chemotherapy drug oxaliplatin against PANC-1 cells 7 . In vivo experiments in
mice bearing subcutaneous PANC-1 tumors confirmed its efficacy, with oral
administration producing tumor reduction comparable to intraperitoneal injections of
oxaliplatin 7 . Crucially, ZQJ29 demonstrated a superior safety profile, with treated mice
showing no significant organ damage or body weight loss, unlike the oxaliplatin-treated
group which exhibited severe toxicity 7 .
Further investigation into ZQJ29's mechanism uncovered a novel molecular target:
Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase 1 (PARP1) 7 . Using Surface Plasmon Resonance analysis,
researchers quantified its binding affinity to PARP1 with a dissociation constant (KD) of
2.86 µM 7 . This discovery links artemisinin's activity to the classic DNA damage
response pathway, as PARP1 is a key enzyme in DNA single-strand break repair. The
antitumor effect of ZQJ29 was shown to be dependent on its inhibition of PARP1, which
led to the upregulation of the tumor suppressor gene TP53 and subsequent
downregulation of the SLC7A11/GPX4 ferroptosis pathway, ultimately promoting cell
death 7 . This identification of a specific protein target provides a clear mechanistic
rationale for its activity and opens avenues for its use in tumors with specific

vulnerabilities, such as those with defective homologous recombination repair (e.g.,
BRCA-mutated cancers).
Beyond simple dimers, pharmacophore hybridization is being used to create "hybrid
molecules" that combine the artemisinin endoperoxide bridge with other bioactive
moieties to generate dual-acting or multi-targeting agents 21 . Examples include ART–
daumone hybrids, which inhibit osteoclast formation, and DHA–coumarin hybrids that
can induce both apoptosis and ferroptosis 21 . Other hybrids, such as DHA–cinnamic acid
conjugates, have been designed to induce autophagy, while ART-derivative-NHC-gold(I)
hybrids have shown strong anticancer activity with high selectivity 21 . This modular
approach allows for the fine-tuning of a compound's biological profile, enabling
researchers to design molecules with optimized potency, selectivity, and mechanisms of
action tailored to specific cancer types. For instance, a synthesized derivative containing a
naphthalene unit showed potent activity against lung cancer cells with an IC50 of 0.09
µM, while other novel ester derivatives were effective against A549 lung cancer cells at
concentrations of 126.3 nM and 138.0 nM, respectively 5 .
The second major innovation wave focuses on nanotechnology-based drug delivery
systems to overcome artemisinin's poor pharmacokinetics. Encapsulating artemisinins
within nanocarriers such as liposomes, polymeric nanoparticles, and other nanocarriers
addresses multiple issues simultaneously 3 . These formulations improve the water
solubility of the otherwise hydrophobic drugs, enhance their stability in circulation, and
prolong their half-life 3 . Most importantly, they facilitate targeted delivery to the tumor
site. Many nanocarriers are engineered to be pH-sensitive, remaining stable in the neutral
pH of the bloodstream but releasing their payload in the slightly acidic tumor
microenvironment, thereby increasing local drug concentration while minimizing
systemic exposure and off-target toxicity 3 .
The impact of these nanoformulations on antitumor efficacy is profound. In vivo studies
have demonstrated markedly superior performance compared to the free drugs. For
example, one study using polyethylene glycol-conjugated DHA nanoparticles reported an
84.6% tumor growth inhibition rate in animal models, whereas the same dose of free
DHA only achieved 29.9% inhibition 3 . This dramatic improvement is attributed to
enhanced cellular uptake and more efficient drug accumulation within the tumor tissue.
The development of mitochondria-targeted artemisinin derivatives, such as conjugates
with triphenylphosphonium (TPP) moieties (e.g., ART-TPP, ARL-TPP), represents another
sophisticated delivery strategy 21 . By directing the drug specifically to the mitochondria
—the powerhouse of the cell and a key organelle in apoptosis—these compounds can
induce a localized and potent burst of ROS, leading to highly efficient cancer cell killing

with IC50 values in the low micromolar range in various cancer cell lines 21 . Fluorescent
coumarin-artemisinin conjugates have also been developed to simultaneously increase
intracellular ROS and induce apoptosis, providing a built-in imaging capability to track
drug distribution and efficacy 21 .

Immunomodulatory Effects and Impact on the Tumor
Microenvironment
Emerging research over the last five years has unveiled a sophisticated and multifaceted
role for artemisinin and its derivatives in modulating the immune system and reshaping
the tumor microenvironment (TME). Beyond their direct cytotoxic effects on cancer cells,
these compounds are increasingly recognized as immunomodulatory agents capable of
stimulating anti-tumor immunity, dismantling immunosuppressive networks, and altering
the behavior of key stromal components within the TME. This evolving understanding
positions artemisinins not merely as chemotherapeutic agents but as potential allies in
the burgeoning field of cancer immunotherapy.
One of the most significant immunomodulatory effects is the ability of artemisinins to
induce immunogenic cell death (ICD), particularly through the induction of ferroptosis
14 19 . ICD is a form of cell death that is not only lethal to the cancer cell but also serves

as an "eat me" signal to the immune system. When cancer cells undergo ICD, they release
damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), which are endogenous molecules that
act as danger signals to alert and activate antigen-presenting cells like dendritic cells 19 .
A study on lung cancer demonstrated that DHA-triggered ferroptosis promotes ICD by
eliciting ER stress and DNA damage, which in turn facilitates the release of DAMPs 19 .
This process enhances the immunogenicity of the tumor, boosting anticancer
immunosurveillance and potentially priming the immune system for a more robust and
sustained response against residual cancer cells 19 . This mechanism is distinct from
traditional apoptosis, which is often immunologically silent or even tolerogenic, making
the ferroptotic pathway a highly desirable route for engaging the immune system.
Artemisinins also appear to directly influence the balance between effector and
regulatory immune cells. They have been shown to regulate immune cell heterogeneity
by triggering a cascade of phosphorylation events involving CDK and MAPK pathways 1 .
Specifically, DHA has been reported to promote the activity of CD8(+) T lymphocytes,
which are critical for directly recognizing and killing cancer cells, while simultaneously
suppressing B cell responses 1 . This shift in the immune landscape can tilt the scales
from an immunosuppressive to an immunostimulatory state within the TME.
Furthermore, artemisinins can remodel the phenotype of tumor-associated macrophages
(TAMs). TAMs often exist in an M2 phenotype, which promotes tumor growth,
angiogenesis, and immunosuppression. DHA has been shown to remodel TAMs into an
anti-tumor M1 phenotype, a process that involves inducing ferroptosis in the TAMs

themselves, leading to DNA damage and NF-κB activation that drives this phenotypic
switch 14 .
Another crucial aspect of artemisinin's immunomodulatory action is its ability to quell the
activity of immunosuppressive cell populations. Regulatory T cells (Tregs) and myeloidderived suppressor cells (MDSCs) are major barriers to effective anti-tumor immunity,
and artemisinins have been shown to counteract their function 21 . In a 4T1 tumor
model, artesunate was found to inhibit prostaglandin production, which in turn
decreased the expression of Foxp3, a key transcription factor for Treg development and
function 21 . By dampening the immunosuppressive influence of Tregs and MDSCs,
artemisinins can help unmask the tumor to the host's immune system, allowing effector T
cells to function more effectively 21 .
The impact of artemisinins extends to the non-cancerous cellular components of the TME,
such as cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs). CAFs are a dominant stromal cell type that
can support tumor progression and confer resistance to therapy. A study in breast cancer
revealed that artemisinin derivatives can inactivate CAFs by suppressing the TGF-β
signaling pathway, a key driver of CAF activation and function 11 . By targeting this
supportive stroma, artemisinins can disrupt the physical and biochemical scaffolding that
sustains the tumor, potentially enhancing the penetration and efficacy of other
therapeutic agents.
Artemisinins also modulate the expression of key immune-related molecules. For
instance, DHA has been shown to restore immunogenicity and enhance anticancer
immunosurveillance of cisplatin-treated cells by activating the PERK/eIF2alpha pathway
1 . In colorectal cancer, artesunate was found to downregulate the levels of

immunosuppressive cytokines TGF-β1 and IL-10 21 . Additionally, artemisinins can inhibit
angiogenesis, a process that is not only vital for tumor growth but also shapes the TME by
influencing immune cell infiltration; by inhibiting VEGF, they can normalize vasculature
and improve immune cell access to the tumor 3 12 .

These findings collectively paint a picture of artemisinins as multi-targeting agents that
can remodel the TME from a hostile, immunosuppressive fortress into a more permissive
environment for anti-tumor immunity. This immunomodulatory capacity provides a
strong rationale for exploring artemisinin-based combination therapies with immune
checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), such as anti-PD-1 or anti-CTLA-4 antibodies. The theory is
that by inducing ICD and reducing immunosuppression, artemisinins could convert "cold"
tumors (non-inflamed, non-responsive to ICIs) into "hot" tumors (inflamed, likely to
respond to ICIs). While this specific combination is more speculative based on the
provided sources, the mechanistic groundwork has been laid. The ability to target not
only the cancer cells but also the surrounding stroma and the infiltrating immune cells
positions artemisinins as uniquely suited for modern, integrated cancer treatment
paradigms that seek to harness the power of the patient's own immune system.

Research Landscape, Future Directions, and Unresolved
Challenges
The five-year period from 2021 to 2026 has been transformative for artemisinin-based
oncology, marking a maturation of the field from initial curiosity about its anticancer
potential to a sophisticated, mechanism-driven research program. The global research

landscape, as analyzed through bibliometric studies, reveals a vibrant and growing
scientific community, with China leading in publication output, followed by the United
States and Germany 8 . The intense focus on topics such as 'ferroptosis', 'nanoparticles',
'combination therapy', and specific cancers like 'lung' and 'colorectal' underscores the key
thematic pillars driving current investigation 8 . As the field moves forward, it is guided
by a clear set of future directions aimed at translating promising preclinical and early
clinical findings into validated clinical practice, while grappling with significant
unresolved challenges.
A comprehensive review of the literature and citation bursts from 2018–2023 highlights
several key future directions for artemisinin-based cancer therapy 8 . The first and most
critical is the expansion and optimization of clinical trials. While Phase I studies have
successfully established safety, there is an urgent need for larger, randomized, and
controlled Phase II and Phase III trials to validate the modest efficacy signals observed in
smaller studies, particularly in the adjuvant setting for cancers like colorectal cancer 3
14 . These trials must be designed to assess hard endpoints like disease-free and overall

survival to provide definitive proof of clinical benefit. A second major direction is the
continued development of optimized drug delivery systems, especially nanoparticles and
other nanocarriers 3 8 . The profound improvements in efficacy and reductions in
toxicity seen in preclinical models with nanoformulations demand their translation into
clinical development to fully realize the therapeutic potential of artemisinins 3 .
Thirdly, there is a concerted effort to elucidate the intricate molecular mechanisms of
action in greater detail and to expand the portfolio of novel derivatives and hybrid
molecules 8 . The discovery of new targets, such as PARP1 for the dimer ZQJ29, opens
new avenues for application and provides a basis for selecting appropriate patient
populations 7 . Finally, a growing focus is being placed on targeting the tumor
microenvironment, including cancer-associated fibroblasts and the immune cell infiltrate,
as a strategy to overcome resistance and enhance the durability of responses 8 11 21 .
The overarching vision is to move away from artemisinin as a simple cytotoxic agent and
toward its use as a precision tool in multimodal regimens, informed by predictive
biomarkers.
Despite this forward momentum, several critical gaps and unresolved challenges remain.
The most significant is the lack of large-scale efficacy data in humans 8 . All claims of
clinical benefit are currently based on preliminary, small-scale studies, and the absence of
positive Phase III trial results means that artemisinins cannot yet be considered standardof-care anticancer agents. Bridging this translational gap is the single most important task
for the field.

A second major challenge is the identification and validation of reliable predictive
biomarkers. The heterogeneous responses to artemisinin therapy suggest that not all
patients or tumors will benefit equally. Research indicates that biomarkers related to iron
metabolism (e.g., high TfR1 expression, high labile iron pools), the ferroptosis pathway
(e.g., low GPX4 or SLC7A11 expression), and p53 status could be indicative of sensitivity
2 14 . However, these hypotheses require rigorous clinical validation in prospective trials

to guide patient selection and ensure that future studies enroll the patients most likely to
respond.
Optimal dosing and scheduling present another set of practical challenges. The ideal
dose, route of administration (oral vs. IV), frequency, and duration of treatment for
different cancer types are largely unknown 8 . While Phase I studies provide starting
points, these parameters will need to be optimized through dose-ranging studies to
maximize efficacy while managing toxicity. The observation that adverse events can
increase with higher oral doses of artesunate highlights the importance of finding the
right therapeutic window for each indication 21 .
The long-term safety profile of chronic oral artemisinin administration is also not fully
understood. While short-to-medium term studies have shown good tolerability, the
potential for cumulative toxicity, particularly concerning the neurological and auditory
systems which have shown transient adverse events in some trials, warrants careful
monitoring in long-term studies 21 . Understanding and mitigating these long-term risks
is essential for applications like chemoprevention or adjuvant maintenance therapy.
Finally, the inevitable development of resistance poses a long-term threat to the utility of
any single-agent therapy. Cancer cells possess remarkable adaptability and can
upregulate antioxidant pathways, alter iron metabolism, or mutate key signaling nodes to
evade cell death 14 . Future research must focus on understanding these resistance
mechanisms and designing rational combination strategies that preempt or overcome
them. For example, combining artemisinins with inhibitors of compensatory survival
pathways could prevent resistance from emerging.
In conclusion, the five-year period from 2021 to 2026 has solidified artemisinin's position
as a compelling and versatile platform for anticancer drug development. The field has
gained a deep mechanistic understanding centered on ferroptosis, initiated the crucial
work of clinical translation with a focus on safety, and launched an innovative second
wave of research focused on novel derivatives and advanced delivery systems. The path
ahead is clear but demanding. It requires a bifurcated approach: on one hand, conducting
rigorous, large-scale clinical trials to validate the promising signals already observed; and

on the other, continuing to leverage cutting-edge chemistry and nanotechnology to
engineer the next generation of artemisinin-based therapeutics. By addressing the critical
challenges of efficacy, biomarker identification, and long-term safety, artemisinin-based
therapies hold the potential to become a meaningful addition to the oncologist's arsenal
in the coming decade.
